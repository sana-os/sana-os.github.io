Post 002 — Variable X · Cognitive Compression

2025

Why we cannot resist deciding who is to blame

The anxiety of the unknown — and how our brains short-circuit it by creating a story.

Have you ever felt a powerful rush of anxiety in the face of something unknown — a strange sound in the night, an unexplained illness, a situation you couldn't read?

The state of not knowing the cause is genuinely intolerable for the human brain. And in that state, we do something almost reflexive: we attach a label. "It must be a ghost." "This must be a terrible disease." The explanation, however wrong, brings immediate relief.

This is the mechanism behind superstition, witch hunts, and scapegoating throughout history. But it is also present in every meeting room, every team conflict, and every moment of everyday friction we dismiss as "personality clashes."

The same reflex in daily life

You have probably experienced versions of this:

  • You feel unwell and immediately search your symptoms online — then convince yourself you have the most alarming diagnosis that fits.
  • A friend you've never had a problem with says something in a tense moment, and suddenly their entire history looks like it was motivated by bad intent.

Neither of these is a character flaw. Both are your brain's defence system doing exactly what it was built to do.

What SANA OS calls Variable X

SANA OS has a name for the unknown that triggers this response: Variable X. It is the gap in your understanding — the unresolved question about why someone is doing what they are doing, or why something is happening the way it is.

Variable X is not a problem to be solved immediately. It is information that needs to be held — temporarily — without being replaced by a story. The brain finds this uncomfortable. That discomfort is the signal that a compression is about to happen.

How this stalls organisations

In a project meeting, three people are looking at the same situation from different positions. The CEO is under pressure from shareholders. The middle manager is chasing this quarter's KPIs. The frontline staff are protecting their workload and their health.

Each of their positions is invisible to the others. That invisibility is a Variable X for everyone in the room.

Before anyone asks, the brain fills in the gap:

  • "Leadership is trying to cut us loose." (assigning malice)
  • "The team has no ownership." (assigning laziness)

This is Cognitive Compression (Survival Compression) — the mechanism that turns an information gap into a verdict before any evidence has been gathered. It is the structural source of blame-seeking that helps no one.

The three layers that reveal what is actually happening

SANA OS organises the pressures people are under into three layers: the survival layer (physical safety and livelihood), the narrative layer (values, belonging, unwritten rules), and the institutional layer (laws, KPIs, formal structures).

When we jump to conclusions about another person's intent, we are almost always responding to a mismatch in which layer each person is prioritising — not to their character.

"Are you deciding someone's intent before you have heard them — because Variable X is too uncomfortable to hold?"

When you can hold the unknown a little longer — when you can stay with "I don't yet know why they are doing this" — the conversation changes. The question shifts from "who is to blame?" to "what is the structural gap here, and how do we close it?"

That shift is what SANA OS is designed to support.
