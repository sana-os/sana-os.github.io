Post 003 — Three Layers · Proximity Rule

2025

Why people follow the atmosphere, not the rules

The three layers that govern human behaviour — and the proximity rule that explains why rational people break formal rules.

We know compliance matters. We know that breaking laws brings consequences, and that ethical violations destroy social trust. And yet the pattern repeats: people follow illegal orders, bend rules under pressure, abandon principles in extreme situations.

Before calling this a failure of character, consider a question: Can you honestly say that the state and its laws matter more to you than the people immediately around you — your family, your colleagues, the community keeping you alive today?

Most of us pause. Because we are built, at a biological level, to prioritise what is near over what is distant.

The three layers

SANA OS organises the pressures that drive human behaviour into three layers, defined by their proximity to survival:

Layer A — Survival. The most immediate layer. Food, sleep, physical safety, and in modern contexts: employment continuity. When this layer is threatened, everything else is secondary. People will do almost anything to restore it.

Layer B — Narrative. The second layer. Belonging, workplace atmosphere, personal pride, unwritten rules. This is the layer that makes you stay late even when the official policy says you can leave — because the atmosphere says otherwise. Layer B is, in a deep sense, what Layer C becomes after enough time passes: yesterday's formal rule, absorbed into today's cultural common sense.

Layer C — Institution. The most distant layer. Laws, KPIs, formal employment regulations. These exist to convert individual survival strategies into collective benefit — but they operate at a remove from our emotional lives. They are real, but they are not present.

The Proximity Rule

The Proximity Rule: Priority = Impact ÷ Distance. Humans prioritise the rule whose punishment or reward is closest in time and space. A distant law is real — but today's atmosphere is immediate.

The employee who follows an unethical order is not ignoring the law. They are rationally adapting to the overwhelming proximity of what they stand to lose: their job (Layer A), their standing in the team (Layer B). The law (Layer C) is real — but it is far away.

When disaster or war strips institutions away, the same logic applies: Layer A (survival) becomes so overwhelming and immediate that it outweighs everything else. This is not moral failure. It is the Proximity Rule operating at its most extreme.

From blame to structural debugging

When you see this through the three-layer model, something shifts in how you read news and workplace conflict.

Attacking an individual for "lack of moral character" when they followed an illegal order changes nothing. What actually needs to be asked is: what was the structural configuration that made following the law more dangerous to their Layer A and B than breaking it?

"The question is never just: what did they do? It is always: what was the structural pressure that made that the rational choice?"

SANA OS makes the layer distribution visible in real conversations — showing where the load is concentrated, and where the gap between formal rule and lived pressure is producing friction. That is where structural debugging begins.
