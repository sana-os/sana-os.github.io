Post 006 — Worksheet 03 · Reform Gap

2025

Why logically correct reforms die in the field

Worksheet 03: diagnosing whether a change will actually be absorbed — before you deploy it.

The reform was correct. Everyone involved had good intentions. The logic was sound. And yet the implementation failed — met with resistance, workarounds, or simply quiet non-adoption.

The instinct is to blame the "resistors." But the structural explanation is more useful: change speed exceeded absorption capacity.

The three structural principles

Legitimacy Translation. Even a structurally correct Layer C rule will be experienced as a foreign object unless it connects to the organisation's existing Layer B narrative. "Use this system from tomorrow because it improves productivity" is a Layer C command. It does nothing to connect with what the team has built its identity around. Without translation, it is rejected.

The Time Layer. Layer C rules can change overnight. Layer B culture changes slowly. When the pace of change exceeds the organisation's absorption capacity, the system breaks — not through dramatic rebellion, but through passive resistance, attrition, and workarounds. The formula: change speed (V) must not exceed structural coefficient (k) × absorption capacity (A_eff).

The Proximity Rule applied to incentives. Frontline staff prioritise immediate, visible consequences over distant formal directives. If the new system increases their workload (Layer A threat) or clashes with how they understand their professional identity (Layer B threat), they will work around it — not out of malice, but out of structural rationality.

Worksheet 03: the pre-deployment diagnostic

The Reform Gap Checklist runs through these structural checks before implementation. It asks:

  • Does this change increase frontline workload or threaten job security? (Layer A check)
  • Does it clash with how the team understands its own identity and pride? (Layer B check)
  • Does it conflict with existing KPIs or formal obligations? (Layer C consistency check)
  • Is the rollout speed within the organisation's absorption capacity? (Time Layer check)

It also requires writing a Legitimacy Translation — a version of the announcement that connects the new rule to what the team already values: "To protect the quality of client relationships we have built, this tool frees up time we currently spend on manual reporting."

"If resistance appears, it is not because people are lazy or obstructive. It means the structural conditions for absorption were not met. That is a design problem, not a character problem."
The closing principle: our responsibility is not to ask "who is resisting?" but "where is the structural friction?" — and to adjust the design accordingly.
