Post 011 — Cognitive Compression · Layer B

2025

Why righteous anger becomes so cruel

Priority seating, online pile-ons, and the structural mechanics of moral compression.

You are on a commuter train. A young person in a suit sits in a priority seat. An elderly person with a cane stands nearby. Something tightens in your chest: why aren't they moving?

You know, rationally, that the person might have an invisible injury. They might be exhausted or unwell. But in that moment, the rational explanation feels unavailable. The discomfort is immediate and strong.

What is actually happening

SANA OS describes this through Layer B: the narrative layer holds the unwritten rules that hold communities together — rules like "yield seats to those who need them more." When you see what looks like a violation, Layer B activates a signal that reads, essentially, as community safety is under threat.

At the same time, the reason for the behaviour is unknown — it is Variable X. The brain cannot sustain the discomfort of Variable X and Layer B alarm simultaneously. So it substitutes: "This person is selfish and rude." The alarm is confirmed. The discomfort is resolved. Dopamine may even be released.

This is Cognitive Compression: Variable X → narrative substitution → verdict. It happens in seconds, and it feels like clarity.

How individual compression becomes collective cruelty

When this individual mechanism is amplified through social media, the dynamic becomes dangerous:

  • Layer B (narrative): "People like this are a threat to the community we share."
  • Layer A (self-protection and belonging): "Joining the condemnation signals my alignment with the community — and protects my standing within it."

The people participating in the pile-on are not, in most cases, motivated by cruelty. They are motivated by Layer B protection — the desire to defend a shared narrative — and Layer A belonging — the desire to be on the right side of the community boundary. The cruelty is the structural output of those pressures, not a character trait of the individuals involved.

The structural question to ask: Is this anger responding to confirmed evidence of bad intent — or is it responding to my Layer B being triggered by an unresolved Variable X?

The practice

Source Trifurcation — one of the foundational tools in Worksheet 04 (History Mystery) — applies here:

  • Fact: a young person is sitting in a priority seat.
  • Perception: my brain's story about why, based on Layer B pressure.
  • Unknown (Variable X): the actual reason.

That separation is not moral relativism. It is structural accuracy. And when it becomes a habit, something changes: the compression slows enough to allow for a question before a verdict.

"When you feel 'I cannot forgive this' — ask: which layer am I protecting? And have I compressed a Variable X into an enemy to protect it?"
