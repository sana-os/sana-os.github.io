Post 012 — GMM · RBM · RSM · Frameworks

2025

Reading the world's health: GMM, RBM, and RSM

Why structural observation needs instruments — and what the three analytical frameworks of SANA OS measure.

The three-layer model and the Proximity Rule explain a great deal about why individuals and teams behave as they do. But when the subject of analysis is a large organisation, an institution, or a state — something more is needed. Subjective observation is not enough. Instruments are required.

This is why SANA OS includes three structural diagnostic frameworks.

RBM: Resource Balance Model

RBM answers one question: where does demand exceed supply?

It maps, for each function in a system, the gap between what is required (Demand) and what is available (Supply). The aggregate structural stress (RBM_Stress = Σ max(Demand_j − Supply_j, 0)) indicates overall load. The bottleneck function — the one with the largest gap — is where intervention is most needed.

The critical reframe RBM provides: overload is not a character problem, it is a supply problem. The person who "can't cope" is almost always rationally responding to a structural imbalance — not exhibiting weakness.

RSM: Role System Model

RSM analyses collective systems not as groups of people but as networks of functions. Six roles are defined — Decider, Architect, Integrator, Executor, Observer, Auditor — each representing a necessary function for system self-correction.

RSM identifies three failure states for each role:

  • Deficit: the role is not adequately filled. The function is missing.
  • Dominance: one role has absorbed the authority of others, suppressing feedback loops.
  • Phantom: the role formally exists but no longer performs its function. The most dangerous state — it produces the illusion of stability.

Historical catastrophes — from military overreach to corporate scandal — frequently show the same RSM pattern: Executor dominance → Observer distortion → Auditor suppression → correction loop failure → systemic collapse.

GMM: Governance Maturity Model

GMM operates at the level of civilisations, states, and large institutions. It diagnoses structural stability across the three layers and asks: can this system absorb the pace of change it is currently facing?

The stability condition: V ≤ k × A_eff (change speed must not exceed the structural coefficient times absorption capacity). When this condition is violated — when institutional change outruns narrative absorption — the system breaks. Not necessarily through drama, but through legitimacy erosion, fragmentation, and eventually dissolution.

GMM is not a prediction tool. It is a diagnostic: where is the load concentrated, and what are the conditions under which the system could remain stable?

What all three frameworks share: they eliminate attribution. "The leader is bad." "The culture is broken." "The people are lazy." These statements shut down analysis. RBM, RSM, and GMM replace them with structural questions that can actually be investigated and addressed.
"These frameworks are not weapons for winning arguments. They are shared maps for people who want to fix the same building — from different rooms, with different tools."
