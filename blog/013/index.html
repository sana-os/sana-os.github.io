Post 013 — History Mystery

2025

Why rational people kept burning witches

History is not a moral verdict. It is a structural record — and the witch trials are one of its clearest lessons.

We look back at the witch trials and feel certain: we would not have done that. We are more educated, more rational, more enlightened than the people who condemned thousands to death for a crime that does not exist.

SANA OS takes a different position. It asks not "were they wrong?" but "what survival system were they adapting to?" The answer is structurally clear — and uncomfortably close to patterns we can observe today.

Step 1: Source Trifurcation

Before analysis, we separate three types of information:

  • Fact: In the 16th–17th centuries, tens of thousands of people were executed as witches across Western Europe.
  • Perception: Contemporary people genuinely believed that crop failures, plague, and the death of children were caused by demonic interference. This perception — not the fact — drove their decisions.
  • Record: The surviving documentation was produced primarily by Church authorities and prosecutors. Record ≠ Fact.

People act on perception, not fact. This is not a historical curiosity. It is the foundational principle of every political movement, every institutional failure, and every misunderstanding we observe today.

Step 2: The three layers of the witch trials

Layer A — survival: The era was one of profound physical crisis. The Little Ice Age had produced repeated harvest failures. Plague had killed millions. War was continuous. The Layer A pressure was total: people were genuinely uncertain whether they would survive the next winter.

Layer C — institution: The formal institutions — the Church, the legal system, secular authority — could not explain or stop the cascading disasters. Layer C had failed to protect Layer A. The institutional adhesive was dissolving.

Layer B — narrative: When Layer C fails to protect Layer A, humans turn to Layer B. The brain cannot sustain the open-ended terror of Variable X — "why are our children dying, why is the harvest failing?" — and substitutes a story. "It is the witches." A cause. An enemy. A corrective action. The story does not require evidence. It requires only that it closes the intolerable gap of not-knowing.

Cognitive Compression at historical scale

This is the Survival Compression mechanism at civilisational scale:

  • Variable X: inexplicable suffering and death.
  • Layer A pressure: total, immediate, lethal.
  • Compression: X → "witches are responsible" → persecution is rational self-defence.

The people who prosecuted witches were not, by the standards of their time, irrational. They were responding rationally to the most extreme survival pressure imaginable, with the cognitive tools available to them, within a narrative framework endorsed by every institutional authority they had access to.

The structural question is not: "How could they believe such a thing?" It is: "Given total Layer A pressure, collapsed Layer C, and a narrative (Layer B) that provided a clear enemy and a clear corrective action — what would the rational response be?"

The contemporary mirror

The witch trials ended. The structure that produced them has not.

When a society faces high Layer A anxiety — economic precarity, health uncertainty, rapid change — and Layer C institutions fail to provide protection or explanation, the Layer B mechanism activates. A cause is identified. An enemy is named. Condemnation of the enemy becomes the rational Layer A survival strategy.

The specific enemy changes. The structure does not.

"Understanding is not agreement. We do not need to endorse the persecution to understand it. But we cannot prevent its modern forms without understanding its structure."
