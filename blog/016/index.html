Why 'justified culling' was ruled illegal — a structural case study

The Sunagawa bear hunting lawsuit: seven years, four courts, and what the verdict reveals about how institutions fail under pressure.

On 27 March 2026, Japan's Supreme Court confirmed a verdict that had taken seven years to reach: a hunter named Ikenoue Haruo was right. The administrative cancellation of his hunting rifle licence was illegal.

The case began in August 2018, when Ikenoue — a deputy member of the Sunagawa City wildlife damage response team — shot and killed a brown bear at close range in response to an official municipal request. A year later, the Hokkaido Public Safety Commission revoked his rifle licence on the grounds that he had fired toward a building.

He had pulled the trigger to protect people. The state labelled him dangerous.

Why did this happen? And what does it reveal about how institutional systems fail under pressure?

Step 1: Zero-Doubt — nobody was wrong, but friction was inevitable

The first principle of SANA OS analysis is Zero-Doubt: every actor was rationally adapting to the environment they were in. Nobody is the villain here. The friction was structural.

The Hunter — Layer A adaptation

Ikenoue Haruo

Faced with a bear at roughly 18 metres in dense vegetation, protecting residents was the immediate survival priority. He made the best judgement available in a situation where Layer A pressure was total and the decision window was seconds.

The Commission — Layer C adaptation

Hokkaido Public Safety Commission

Their function in the system is Auditor — guardian of the firearms act. Given the physical facts (ricochet, building proximity), their algorithm had only two outputs: revoke or overlook. They applied the law they were designed to apply.

The Municipality — Phantom Architect

Sunagawa City Administration

They created the response team structure and made the request. But they never built the legal protection shield, the clear firing protocols, or the prior alignment with police and the Commission. The role of Architect was structurally absent.

The Police Officer — Proximity Rule

Officer B at the scene

Rather than invoking the firearms law, the officer guided residents to safety — tacitly enabling the bear to be shot. Immediate Layer A survival pressure outweighed the distant Layer C rule. This is not contradiction. It is the Proximity Rule in operation.

Step 2: What happens when you search for a culprit

The instinct when something goes wrong is to identify the person responsible and punish them. Punish the hunter for firing unsafely. Punish the Commission for bureaucratic rigidity. Punish the police for contradicting themselves.

But if blame is assigned and a scapegoat is identified, the underlying structural bugs remain untouched:

  • The chronic undersupply of trained personnel — the system's dependence on the goodwill of ageing volunteer hunters.
  • The absence of legal protection for those carrying out public safety work at personal risk.
  • The gap between what the law was designed for (personal hunting) and what it was being applied to (emergency public service).

The same situation will recur. A different hunter in the same structural position will face the same impossible calculation.

"Punishment ends the conversation. Structural diagnosis continues it — and prevents the next failure."

Step 3: The Supreme Court's structural intervention

The Supreme Court's 2026 ruling was not merely a change in legal interpretation. It was a structural intervention — a patch applied to a system at risk of total failure.

The court's reasoning connected two separate Layer C instruments: the firearms act (designed to prevent harm from personal weapons use) and the Wildlife Damage Prevention Special Measures Act (designed to enable communities to protect themselves). By reading these together, the court reframed Ikenoue's action from "personal hunting" to "public safety function performed as a non-permanent public official."

The ruling also explicitly identified the RBM risk: continuing the revocation would "have a chilling effect on the willingness of private citizens to serve as team members — potentially leading to outcomes contrary to the purpose of the Special Measures Act." In structural terms: if the punishment is maintained, Supply (hunters willing to serve) collapses, and Layer A (public safety) fails.

Justice Watanabe's concurring opinion went further, calling for proportionality mechanisms — the ability to distinguish between degrees of fault rather than choosing only between revocation and impunity. This is a call for Governance Maturity: a more sophisticated Layer C that can handle the complexity of the situations it governs.

The question for your organisation

When something goes wrong in your team, do you identify a responsible person, apply a punishment, and consider the matter closed?

If so, the structural bottleneck that caused the problem is still there — quietly accumulating pressure. The next failure is already in progress.

Understanding is not agreement. We do not need to endorse the Commission's decision to understand it. But we cannot prevent the next version of this situation without understanding the structural forces that made everyone's behaviour inevitable — and without redesigning those structures accordingly.

Extended Analysis — Research Documentation

The following is a full structural analysis of the case using SANA OS frameworks (CPM, GMM, RBM, RSM). It is intended for researchers and practitioners who want to examine the case at framework depth.

Historical background: the structural accumulation (GMM + Time Layer)

The 1990 abolition of spring bear culling in Hokkaido — driven by near-extinction of the species in some areas — represented a Layer B value (ecological conservation) becoming Layer C policy. This was structurally sound. But the Time Layer analysis reveals the delayed consequence: as bear populations recovered and human settlement patterns shifted (rural depopulation, abandoned farmland, loss of buffer zones), bear–human contact zones expanded. The system adapted its values successfully but failed to adapt its institutional capacity at the same rate.

The result, through RBM: Demand (emergency culling requests) grew exponentially. Supply (trained, available, legally protected responders) stagnated and declined through ageing. The Gap — entirely borne by volunteer hunters serving as non-permanent public officials — became chronically unsustainable before any single incident occurred.

CPM: cognitive state transitions at the scene

At the moment of arrival, Ikenoue's cognitive state was Active — exploratory simulation. He immediately assessed the bear as a young cub and proposed releasing it, demonstrating high uncertainty tolerance and professional scenario modelling.

The environmental trigger shifted when the municipal official conveyed residents' sustained anxiety and insisted on culling. Survival Anxiety entered the system from outside — transmitted through the administrative chain into the hunter's immediate decision environment. When the bear crossed the road at 18 metres in dense vegetation, the mode completed its transition to Survival Compression: rapid, experience-based, irreversible decision under lethal proximity pressure.

The parallel failure: Officer B on the scene was also in Survival Compression — but his was socially oriented. He resolved the ambiguity of the situation by doing what was immediately viable (guiding residents to safety) rather than what was legally required (halting the operation). This is not hypocrisy. It is Proximity Rule operating as designed.

RSM: role overload and the Phantom Architect

In the RSM framework, Ikenoue's assigned role was Executor. But at the scene, he was simultaneously functioning as Decider (should I fire?), Integrator (where is the other team member?), and Observer (what is behind the target?). The concentration of three high-cognitive-demand roles in a single node under Survival Compression conditions is the structural source of the incident — not personal error.

The Supreme Court's Justice Watanabe identified this precisely: questioning whether it was reasonable to expect someone facing a bear at close range to simultaneously assess distance to multiple buildings they could not clearly see.

The Phantom Architect: Sunagawa City created the institutional structure (the response team), made the operational request (calling Ikenoue to the scene), and then provided none of the architectural support that structure required — no pre-established firing protocols, no legal indemnification, no prior coordination with the police authority that would later judge the action. The role of system designer existed on paper and was absent in practice.

Three structural proposals

1. Layer C update — explicit emergency culling framework. The firearms act and wildlife protection law must be amended to define emergency public culling as a distinct operational category with its own safety standards and proportionate administrative response options — not binary revocation/impunity.

2. Phantom Architect resolution — municipal liability architecture. Municipalities that activate response teams must provide legal indemnification, incident insurance, and pre-established operational protocols. The Executor role cannot bear structural risks that belong to the Architect.

3. Premise Alignment institutionalisation. Before incidents occur, all stakeholders — hunters, police, public safety commissioners, municipal officials — should establish shared operational premises: what constitutes a valid emergency, what constitutes acceptable risk, who makes which decisions. This is the Dialogue Operating System applied to public safety infrastructure.
