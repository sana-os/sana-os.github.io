Post 029 — Case Study · Economic History

Japan · Energy Arc

The 1973 oil shock: how Japan's entire industrial configuration was built on a single assumption — and what happened when that assumption failed

The first oil shock as a four-framework case study: the CPM puzzle of toilet paper, the RSM activation of the Nikaidō Statement, and the structural transformation that followed.

On 6 October 1973, Egyptian and Syrian forces crossed into Israeli-held territory. Within days, the Arab members of OPEC — the Organisation of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries — announced a production cut and embargo targeting nations deemed to support Israel. Japan imported 99% of its oil. Most came from the Middle East.

What followed is one of the most analytically rich episodes in modern Japanese history — not because of the damage, but because of how the system responded. The same four lenses used throughout this series reveal a configuration under extreme stress, a correction loop that activated after initial failure, and a structural transformation that shaped Japanese industry for the next two decades.

RBM: the supply line that was never secure

Japan's post-war economic growth had been built on a structural assumption: cheap, abundant oil from the Middle East would remain available. By 1973, Japan imported approximately 99% of its crude oil, with the overwhelming majority from Arab nations. The domestic energy self-sufficiency rate had fallen to around 8.5%.

In RBM terms, the energy supply function (j) had been maximally optimised for demand. The "heavy, thick, long, large" (重厚長大) industrial model — steel, chemicals, shipbuilding, petrochemicals — consumed energy in enormous quantities and had been configured on the assumption that Demand_j would always be met. There was no structural buffer. Short_j was close to zero only because Supply_j had been treated as a constant.

When OAPEC announced production cuts and the embargo, Supply_j fell sharply and suddenly. The resulting Short_j was not a probability — it was an immediate, physical reality. Factories faced potential shutdown. Power rationing began. The entire industrial base was operating at the edge of viability.

The oil price increase compounded the resource shortage. OPEC raised the official price of crude from $3.01 to $5.12 per barrel in October, then to $11.65 in December — a quadrupling within two months. Japan's 1974 consumer price inflation reached 23%. Real GDP growth fell to −1.2% — Japan's first post-war negative growth.

CPM: the toilet paper mystery

Here is the analytical puzzle. In late October 1973, supermarkets across Japan were stripped of toilet paper. Customers lined up before opening time. Fights broke out over supplies. The panic spread to detergent, sugar, salt, and other daily necessities.

Toilet paper has no direct relationship to crude oil. Japan was not running out of paper. So why did an oil embargo cause a toilet paper panic?

CPM · Cognitive Compression Case

The toilet paper panic of October 1973

The origin: a rumour circulated in late October in Senri New Town (Osaka) — a newly developed suburb with full flush toilet infrastructure — that "paper" would run out. On 19 October, the Minister of Trade and Industry Nakasone publicly called for paper conservation. This government announcement, intended to signal awareness of the crisis, instead functioned as official confirmation of scarcity. The Variable X — an incomprehensible geopolitical crisis — was processed through Survival Compression: the brain, unable to simulate the complex chain from Middle Eastern oil to Japanese industrial output, compressed the threat into the most immediate, verifiable action available. If paper is scarce, buy paper now. The neighbours are already at the store. The social proof — visible queue length — amplified the conformity pressure. By 1 November, over 200 people were queuing before opening at a Senri supermarket for toilet paper priced at ¥138. By peak panic, the same product sold for ¥380. The panic spread to unrelated goods through the same mechanism: once Narrative Lock set in, any shortage signal confirmed the threat, regardless of logical connection to oil.

What the panic illustrates: physical supply was not actually short. Japan was not running out of toilet paper. The panic created the shortage it feared. This is the self-fulfilling prophecy pattern that CPM identifies as the most dangerous output of Narrative Lock: when a narrative of scarcity is locked in, the collective behaviour it produces creates the scarcity it assumed.

GMM: all three layers under simultaneous stress

The oil shock did not damage one layer of the social system. It hit all three simultaneously, in sequence.

Layer A (survival) took the first direct impact: physical energy supply was cut. Factories could not operate. Transport was constrained. The basic functioning of industrial society was threatened.

Layer C (institutions) failed in its stabilising function almost immediately. Consumer prices rose 23% in 1974. The market price mechanism — the primary institutional tool for allocating scarce resources — produced panic buying and hoarding rather than rational adjustment. The existing regulatory framework had no mechanism to address this configuration.

Layer B (narrative) suffered the most durable damage. The dominant post-war narrative — "economic growth is permanent, resources are unlimited, Japan will always get more prosperous" — collapsed. The psychological foundation of the high-growth era was gone. What replaced it was a combination of anxiety and, eventually, a new and more resilient identity: Japan as a resource-constrained nation that survives through efficiency.

RSM: the correction loop that activated — but only after initial failure

Japan's initial governance response to the crisis showed exactly the RSM failure pattern familiar from this series: the Observer and Auditor functions had been systematically underweight during the growth period. No one had been systematically watching the Middle East risk. No early warning system existed. The correction loop was not ready.

But the correction loop activated — rapidly and decisively — once the crisis made inaction impossible.

RSM Correction Loop Activation — October to December 1973

Oct 1973
Crisis onset. OAPEC production cuts. MITI-MFA internal conflict: MITI (Decider/Integrator, supply-focused) pushes for pro-Arab diplomatic pivot; MFA (Observer/Auditor, alliance-focused) resists. Production loop's survival pressure overrides correction loop's alliance maintenance.
22 Nov 1973
二階堂談話 (Nikaidō Statement). The government declares: Israeli withdrawal from all occupied territories must occur; Palestinian rights must be respected; Japan will reconsider its policy toward Israel if the situation does not improve. This is a direct rupture with the US-aligned diplomatic posture Japan had maintained since 1945 — an institutional rewrite at Layer C driven entirely by Layer A survival requirements.
14–18 Dec 1973
三木特使派遣 (Miki Mission). Deputy Prime Minister Miki Takeo visits Arab nations. Japan offers ¥38 billion for Suez Canal expansion, ¥27.7 billion for Syrian refinery construction. The Integrator role: converting the Decider's policy shift into concrete economic relationships that the Executor can sustain.
25 Dec 1973
Japan designated "friendly nation." OAPEC removes Japan from the embargo list. The correction loop has succeeded: Layer A (oil supply) is restored. The cost was a fundamental change to Layer C (diplomatic posture) and Layer B (the narrative of US-alignment as identity).

Simultaneously, the government enacted two emergency laws in December 1973 to control the domestic panic. The Emergency Measures Law against Hoarding and Withholding allowed the government to designate specific goods and order their sale — with criminal penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment or ¥1 million fine for non-compliance. The National Life Stabilisation Emergency Measures Law gave government authority to set standard prices and impose levies on excess pricing.

These laws represent the Layer C institutional rewrite that the crisis required: the government creating new rules to force the market to behave in a way that the existing rules could not produce under panic conditions.

The long-term transformation

The structural transformation that followed the crisis is where the analysis becomes most interesting. The oil shock was not only a disaster — it was a forcing function that produced outcomes Japan could not have achieved through planned industrial policy.

The "heavy, thick, long, large" industries that had powered growth — steel, chemicals, shipbuilding — were revealed to be structurally vulnerable. The industries that replaced them — automobiles, electronics, semiconductors, precision equipment — were energy-efficient, knowledge-intensive, and highly exportable. This transition, which Japanese economists call the shift from 重厚長大 to 軽薄短小 (light, thin, short, small), was not planned. It was driven by the structural necessity of surviving in a high-energy-cost environment.

The cultural transformation was equally durable. "Mottainai" (もったいない — roughly, "don't waste") became a national value rather than an old-fashioned habit. Energy efficiency became a source of competitive pride rather than a burden. Japan's industrial culture was permanently shaped by the experience of an energy crisis that revealed the fragility underneath the growth.

The structural insight that Post 030 will develop: the transformation produced by the first oil shock gave Japan the cognitive and institutional tools to handle the second one — in 1979 — far more effectively than the US or Western Europe. The correction loop, once activated and embedded in new laws and industrial practice, became a permanent feature of the system rather than a temporary crisis response.

Post 030

Six years later, the same country faced the same threat again: another oil shock, another supply crisis, another price explosion. Japan's response in 1979 was radically different from 1973 — and the difference reveals exactly what a successfully embedded correction loop looks like.
