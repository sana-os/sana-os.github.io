Post 035 — Comparative Analysis · Financial History

Japan · Financial Arc

Nomura and Yamaichi: the structure of 'barbaric courage'

Nomura survived 1997 not through superior virtue but through a cultural incentive structure that rewarded the action the situation required — and an RSM configuration that delivered market signals to the people who could act on them.

Nomura Securities survived 1997 for the same reason that a doctor with strong nerves can amputate without flinching: the culture rewarded the action, and the structure made the information visible.

Nomura had losses in the bubble collapse. Nomura had compliance failures — the same 1997 total-shakai scandal touched Nomura as it touched Yamaichi. Nomura was not immune to the era's problems. What was different was the configuration that determined how those problems were processed.

The cultural root: "barbaric courage" (蛮族的な胆力)

The term used internally at Nomura to describe what distinguished their best performers is precise and deliberately unflattering: 蛮族的な胆力 — barbaric courage, or the courage of a barbarian. The connotation is someone who acts without the social inhibitions that prevent decisive action: someone who will bow their head to the floor and beg a client's forgiveness for losses, then immediately execute the loss-cut that stops the bleeding.

Yamaichi's Layer B identity was "法人の山一" — the Yamaichi of corporate relationships. The cultural premium was on maintaining relationships and protecting face. Acknowledging losses to a client was a cultural violation, not a business decision.

Nomura's Layer B identity was numerical: producing results, hitting targets, earning through performance. The cultural premium was on the score, not the process. A salesperson who generated revenue was valued regardless of their academic background. A salesperson who failed to generate revenue was not valued regardless of their academic background.

Nomura

加点方式 (merit addition)

Performance accumulates positively. Revenue generated is the primary signal. Loss-cutting a client position, however painful, produces the revenue outcome needed for the next trade. Barbaric courage is culturally rewarded.

Yamaichi

減点方式 (demerit subtraction)

Status erodes through visible failures. Telling a client about losses is a failure. Not telling them and managing the problem through tobashi preserves surface status. Cultural penalty for transparency.

The difference is not moral. Both systems were rational responses to their own internal incentive configurations. But only Nomura's system allowed market reality — "these assets are worth less than recorded" — to become an operational fact that drove action.

RSM: the Executor who reaches the Decider

Nomura's RSM configuration is the structural complement to its cultural configuration. Where Yamaichi's Shayūkai suppressed the Executor role, Nomura's culture elevated it.

Nomura's sales staff — the retail branches, the direct-client relationship managers — were the organisation's heroes. Promotion came from performance. The organisation chart moved you upward if you sold well. The signals that reached the Decider level therefore included the real-time experience of what was happening in client portfolios: losses were visible, resistance was reportable, and the market's refusal to recover was a fact that entered the correction loop.

When the bubble collapsed and Nomura's corporate clients were sitting on massive losses, Nomura's sales force was the mechanism that transmitted this reality upward. The Decider function received actual market signals rather than internally-filtered reports. The decision to execute loss-cuts — however painful — was made with accurate information about what was happening.

Yamaichi's correction loop received the information it was structurally permitted to receive: the Shayūkai's interpretation of events, filtered through the faction's survival interests. The tobashi continued because the information that would have forced a different decision was never allowed to reach the level where that decision could be made.

CPM: exploratory simulation vs narrative lock

The CPM contrast is the most direct. Both organisations faced the same Variable X: "asset prices may not recover for years or decades." This was genuinely unknown — Japan had never experienced sustained asset price deflation in the post-war period.

Nomura's response was what CPM calls exploratory simulation: the firm held the uncomfortable possibility open, ran the forward scenarios including the sustained-deflation case, and arrived at the structural discovery that the loss-cut was the only path to organisational survival. Then it executed — painfully, with apologies, with client relationships at risk — because the simulation had shown that not executing would be worse.

Yamaichi's response was narrative substitution: "markets will recover, the Ministry of Finance will protect major institutions, our relationships will hold." Each of these propositions was plausible in isolation and had been true at various points in the past. The combination became a Narrative Lock — a configuration so stable that contradicting evidence was processed as noise rather than signal.

"Nomura did not survive 1997 because it was more virtuous than Yamaichi. It survived because its cultural incentive structure rewarded the action that the market situation required — and because its RSM configuration allowed market signals to reach the people who could act on them."
The structural lesson: what Nomura had that Yamaichi did not is specific — not size (Nomura was larger, but size alone does not explain the correction), not compliance (both had compliance failures), and not ethics (the individuals involved were not categorically different in their moral reasoning). What Nomura had was a cultural reward system that pointed in the same direction as the correction the situation required, and an RSM configuration that delivered accurate information to the Decider level. Both are structural features, not personal qualities.

Daiwa Securities had a different problem from Nomura: it had losses it could not absorb independently. Its solution was structural — import an external correction function by forming an alliance with Sumitomo Bank. What does it mean to import a governance capability you cannot build internally?
