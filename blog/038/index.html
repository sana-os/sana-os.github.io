Japan · Financial Arc

Financial Big Bang: what the comparison of four firms reveals about Japan's paradigm shift

The Big Bang did not select for efficient firms — it selected for firms with correction capacity. The structural logic of a system rewrite that cannot be performed while the system is operating.

Posts 034 through 037 examined four securities firms through the same 1997 storm. Nomura survived through internal correction capacity. Daiwa imported an external Auditor via the Sumitomo Bank alliance. Nikko weaponised fear to break its conformity lock, then rebuilt with US governance architecture. Yamaichi could not correct, could not import, could not break — and dissolved.

This post asks the next question: what was the storm, structurally? What was the Financial Big Bang actually doing to the system — and why did it produce the specific configuration in which correction capacity became the survival variable?

What the Big Bang actually was

The "日本版金融ビッグバン" was announced by the second Hashimoto Cabinet in November 1996. Its target date was 2001. Its three declared principles were: Free (market-driven), Fair (transparent and trustworthy), Global (internationally competitive). Its explicit model was the 1986 Thatcher Big Bang that had restored London's status as a global financial centre.

But the structural reality of what the Big Bang was doing can be stated more precisely: it was converting Japan's financial system from a discretion-based governance model to a rule-based governance model — and doing so on a compressed timeline, in a system that was simultaneously trying to process the largest accumulation of non-performing loans in modern financial history.

The "convoy system" (護送船団方式) that the Big Bang was dismantling had performed a specific function: it ensured that even the weakest financial institution could not fail, by keeping industry-wide margins high enough to subsidise the weak. The Ministry of Finance administered this not through published rules but through administrative guidance — informal instructions that carried legal force through the threat of licence withdrawal.

In GMM terms, this was Layer C operating entirely through Layer B mechanisms: the rules were real, but they were transmitted through relationship networks and understood norms rather than through codified statute. The Big Bang was, in the most literal sense, a rewrite of Japan's financial Layer C.

The foreign exchange law: the irreversible accelerator

Of all the Big Bang's reforms, the revision of the Foreign Exchange and Foreign Trade Law (外為法), implemented in April 1998, was the one that made all others non-negotiable.

Before 1998, Japanese individuals and corporations could only conduct foreign currency transactions through designated foreign exchange banks (為銀). Cross-border capital flows required approval. The system maintained a structural asymmetry: Japanese savers' money stayed in Japan because it had to.

After April 1998, any individual or institution could hold accounts at foreign banks, invest directly in foreign assets, and transfer capital internationally without restriction. The domestic financial system was no longer the only available container for Japanese savings.

The structural implication was immediate and permanent: if Japanese financial institutions could not offer competitive products, Japanese capital would leave. The threat of capital flight converted every other Big Bang reform from "desirable policy" to "survival requirement." You cannot reverse liberalise a foreign exchange regime once capital has become mobile — the attempt would itself trigger the capital flight you were trying to prevent.

This is the RBM mechanism: the external liberalisation created a Demand_j (competitive financial products, transparency, risk-adjusted returns) that the existing Supply_j (protected margins, opaque administrative guidance, limited product range) could not meet. The Gap was not closable by adjustment within the old system. The old system had to be replaced.

The simultaneous wars: why the Big Bang produced a paradox

Here is the structural contradiction that the comparison of the four securities firms illuminates.

The Big Bang's logic was: expose financial institutions to market discipline, let the weak fail, let the strong capture their market share, and the resulting industry will be globally competitive. This is coherent as a theory of competitive selection.

But the Big Bang was implemented at exactly the moment when Japan's financial institutions were carrying approximately ¥100 trillion in non-performing loans — the accumulated debris of the bubble collapse that had been deferred, restructured, and partially hidden for nearly a decade.

The government was therefore attempting two simultaneous operations that pointed in opposite directions:

OperationLogicStructural requirement
Big Bang liberalisationMarket selection: let weak institutions fail, reward strong onesNo bailouts; losses are real; capital is mobile; governance is transparent
NPL crisis managementSystem preservation: prevent systemic collapse that would destroy the real economyPublic capital injection; managed failure sequencing; prevent panic runs

The contradiction was not a policy error. It was a structural necessity: without the NPL management, the Big Bang's market discipline would have produced simultaneous failure of most major financial institutions, destroying the payment system and credit allocation function of the entire economy. Without the Big Bang's liberalisation, the protected system would have continued deferring its losses indefinitely, as it had been doing since 1990.

The result was that the government liberalised and injected public capital simultaneously. The Big Bang declared that market selection would determine which institutions survived — and then provided ¥70 trillion in public capital to ensure that the selection process did not kill the entire system. This is not hypocrisy. It is the structural logic of a system rewrite that cannot be performed while the system is operating.

What the four-firm comparison reveals about the Big Bang

The Nomura/Daiwa/Nikko/Yamaichi comparison reveals that the Big Bang's market selection function did operate — but not in the way its designers expected.

The intended selection mechanism was competitive: firms that offered better products at lower cost would capture market share; firms that could not would shrink or fail. This is the standard liberalisation logic.

What actually happened was governance selection. The firms that survived were those whose internal governance structure — their RSM configuration — was capable of processing the correction that the Big Bang's new rules required. The firms that could not perform this correction failed, regardless of their size or historical position.

Nomura

Internal correction capacity

Culture rewarded loss-cutting. Market signals reached the Decider. No external input needed — the internal correction loop was functional under pressure.

Daiwa

External correction import

Internal capacity insufficient. Imported it via Sumitomo Bank alliance. The external Auditor made correction possible that internal culture could not produce.

Nikko

Lock-break + external import

Conformity lock broken by weaponising Yamaichi fear. US governance architecture imported via Travelers. Two-stage: break, then rebuild.

Yamaichi had none of these pathways available. Its internal culture suppressed correction. Its leadership could not identify or execute an external alliance before the liquidity crisis closed the window. Its conformity pressure could not be broken because the Moya architecture had already committed the institution to a path from which correction was structurally impossible by the time the urgency became undeniable.

The Big Bang did not cause Yamaichi's collapse. But it created the conditions under which Yamaichi's existing governance failure — the seven-year accumulation of hidden losses — became unsurvivable. The new rules required that losses be made visible. The new capital requirements ensured that visible losses could not be indefinitely deferred. The new competitive environment meant that institutions without correction capacity could not be indefinitely protected.

"The Financial Big Bang did not select for the most efficient financial institutions. It selected for institutions whose internal governance structure was capable of processing the correction that market transparency required. Governance capacity, not competitive efficiency, was the survival variable."

What the Big Bang did not produce — and why

The Big Bang's declared goal was to make Tokyo a financial centre comparable to New York and London. By 2001, this had not occurred, and the gap has not substantially closed since.

The structural reason is specific. The Big Bang succeeded in one of its two tasks: it dismantled the convoy system, forced transparency, and cleared the hidden losses that the old regime had been deferring. This is a genuine achievement — the NPL problem that took Japan nearly a decade to acknowledge was finally processed.

But the Big Bang's second task — attracting international capital and building globally competitive financial institutions — was impeded by exactly the same public capital injection that made the first task survivable. The institutions that received public capital were not forced through the competitive selection that would have produced genuinely globally capable survivors. They were preserved, restructured through mergers (the "megabank" consolidation), and made domestically stable — but not globally aggressive.

The ¥1,200 trillion in personal financial assets that the Big Bang was designed to mobilise remained largely in savings accounts and postal savings — not because the liberalisation failed, but because the simultaneous financial crisis caused Japanese households to retrench from risk precisely when the system was being opened to it. The CPM dynamic: the cognitive compression produced by the 1997 crisis (Yamaichi's collapse, the Hokkaido Takushoku Bank failure, the global contagion from the Asian financial crisis) moved household financial behaviour toward safety at the exact moment the Big Bang was trying to shift it toward risk.

The structural summary of the financial arc (Posts 034–038): The Big Bang was a necessary systemic rewrite — the convoy model could not survive capital mobility, and capital mobility was non-negotiable once the foreign exchange reform was implemented. The rewrite revealed which institutions had governance structures capable of processing correction and which did not. Three did; one did not. The rewrite also produced an unavoidable paradox: market discipline required the failure of institutions that could not correct, but systemic stability required that the failure be managed rather than allowed to cascade. Both requirements were structurally real, and both were pursued simultaneously. The result was a financial system that was substantially more transparent and rule-based than its predecessor — but not the global financial centre that the three declared principles had envisioned.

What comes next

The financial arc has examined Japan's post-bubble institutional reform through the lens of four firms and one transformative policy. The same Japan — the one that had survived two oil shocks, built a manufacturing-efficiency empire, and then navigated the destruction of its own financial system — was simultaneously undergoing a different kind of institutional reform.

The International Peace Cooperation Law of 1992 redefined what Japanese institutions — specifically the Self-Defence Forces — were legally permitted to do outside Japan's borders. That redefinition had consequences that extended far beyond foreign policy: it shaped the legal architecture that determined what the SDF could and could not do when Japan's own cities were destroyed — starting with Kobe in January 1995.
