Japan · Institutional Reform Arc

Postal privatisation: ¥350 trillion, an inverted shortage, and the election that simplified everything

How Japan Post became the world's largest deposit institution — and why dismantling the pipeline required a referendum rather than a structural argument.

The Financial Big Bang (Post 038) dismantled the "convoy system" that had protected Japan's financial institutions since the post-war period. But there was another convoy system — larger, older, and more deeply embedded in the political economy — that the Big Bang had not touched.

At its peak in the late 1990s, Japan Post held approximately ¥350 trillion in individual deposits and insurance funds. This made it the world's largest financial institution by deposits. The money did not sit in a vault. It flowed — automatically, through the Ministry of Finance's Fiscal Investment and Loan Programme (財政投融資, FILP) — into government bonds and quasi-governmental special corporations: the Japan Highway Public Corporation, the Housing Loan Corporation, the Urban Development Corporation, and dozens of others.

The structural question the postal privatisation was answering was not "should post offices be private?" It was: what happens when a supply system generates more capital than its intended recipients can rationally absorb?

RBM: the inverted shortage

The standard RBM analysis diagnoses systems where Demand exceeds Supply — where the need outstrips the capacity to meet it. Japan's postal savings system produced the opposite configuration: Supply so far exceeded rational Demand that the excess created its own artificial demand.

The mechanism was straightforward. Special corporations — entities that existed partly to absorb FILP capital — had no strong market discipline requiring them to deploy capital efficiently. They could borrow at below-market rates from the postal system, invest in projects that would have been rejected by commercial lenders, and rely on implicit government guarantees to obscure the cost of their underperformance.

The result was visible in the projects: highways with traffic volumes a fraction of their projected use; resorts in remote locations that no one visited; bridges to islands with a few hundred residents. These were not primarily the products of corrupt intent. They were the structural output of a system in which the supply of capital was detached from the discipline of return — where Sat_j (the sufficiency ratio) was artificially maintained at 1.0 by political allocation rather than market assessment.

In this configuration, privatising Japan Post was not primarily about competition in the postal or banking market. It was about interrupting the automatic flow of ¥350 trillion from household savings into projects whose evaluation was political rather than economic.

RSM: the external Auditor that arrived as annual homework

The structural pressure that accelerated the postal privatisation came from an unexpected source: the US-Japan Regulatory Reform and Competition Policy Initiative — specifically, the Annual Reform Recommendations (年次改革要望書) submitted by the US government to Japan each autumn from 1993 onward.

In RSM terms, this was an externally-imposed Auditor function. The Japanese government, which had been operating its FILP system largely free from external governance scrutiny, found itself receiving annual "homework" from Washington: specific recommendations on postal reform, insurance market opening, legal service liberalisation, land use regulation, and dozens of other domains.

The mechanism was precise. Each autumn, the US submitted its requests. The following spring, the US Trade Representative published the National Trade Estimate Report on Foreign Trade Barriers — effectively scoring Japan's compliance. The sequence created a governance accountability loop that operated outside Japan's domestic political system.

The postal recommendations were particularly pointed: the US requested equal competitive conditions (イコールフッティング) between Japan Post and private financial institutions — equal taxation, equal regulatory treatment, equal supervisory standards. The implicit demand was that the government-backed distribution network for ¥350 trillion in capital should not compete on preferential terms against private sector institutions, including US insurers seeking access to the Japanese market.

The RSM Role Coupling diagnosis: the annual reform requests created a structural dependency in which Japan's domestic policy decisions were partially determined by the outputs of an external governance system. This is not a moral verdict — it is a structural observation. The Japanese political system had a domestic Auditor deficit for postal reform (powerful LDP factions with deep ties to the postmaster network had blocked reform for decades). The US annual requests filled that Auditor gap from outside. The same dynamic appeared in the financial Big Bang: external pressure (capital mobility, global competition) made domestic reform non-negotiable in a way that domestic Auditor functions alone had not achieved.

CPM: "postal privatisation, yes or no" — the simplification that moved ¥350 trillion

The actual postal privatisation was not accomplished by structural analysis. It was accomplished by Koizumi Jun'ichirō's cognitive architecture — specifically, by his construction of the 2005 general election as a single-issue referendum.

By 2005, postal reform had been blocked for decades. The resistance was structural: approximately 240,000 post office employees, a nationwide network of postmasters with deep local political ties, and an LDP faction system in which rural constituencies depended on the postal network as employment infrastructure. The complexity of the policy — five separate entities, separation of banking from insurance from delivery from counter services, transitional arrangements, regulatory framework — was genuine and substantial.

Koizumi's cognitive intervention was to refuse this complexity. When the House of Councillors rejected the privatisation bill in August 2005, he dissolved the House of Representatives — an act with no constitutional precedent in that context — and framed the election as a direct question to the electorate: "Are you for postal privatisation, or against it?"

In CPM terms, this was Narrative Simplification deployed as a political instrument: compressing a multi-dimensional policy decision into a binary that bypassed the conventional deliberative process. The LDP members who had voted against the bill were labelled "assassins" and denied official party endorsement. Alternative candidates ("assassin hunters," 刺客) were sent to run against them in their own constituencies.

The result was the LDP's largest electoral victory in decades — 296 seats in the lower house. The Senate having already rejected the bill, the election result was treated as a mandate to repassa it. The bill passed in October 2005. Japan Post was split into four entities and partially privatised.

"Koizumi did not win the argument about postal privatisation. He changed the format of the argument — from a policy debate to a referendum — and won that instead. The ¥350 trillion shifted, not because the structural case was conclusively made, but because the cognitive architecture of the election made opposition structurally impossible."

GMM: the structural contradiction that privatisation did not resolve

The postal privatisation succeeded as a political event. Its structural outcomes are more ambiguous.

The FILP reform that preceded privatisation — the 2001 abolition of the mandatory deposit requirement that had automatically channelled postal savings into government accounts — was the more consequential structural change. From 2001, Japan Post could invest in market instruments rather than being required to deposit with the Ministry of Finance. The automatic pipeline from household savings to special corporations was interrupted.

But the GMM configuration that had generated the ¥350 trillion did not change. Japanese households continued to prefer low-risk, liquid savings instruments. The postal network continued to offer them. The capital continued to accumulate, now managed by Japan Post Bank rather than flowing automatically to FILP — but still seeking somewhere to go in an environment of near-zero interest rates.

The subsequent history confirms this. Japan Post Bank, established in 2007, became one of the largest holders of Japanese government bonds — channelling deposits into the same government debt that FILP had previously funded through a different mechanism. The pipeline was interrupted; the flow found a new channel.

More structurally significant: from approximately 2012 onward, Japan Post began a gradual reversal — what analysts have called "官業回帰" (return to public enterprise). Counter services were expanded. Network coverage was maintained in rural areas where private services did not reach. Post office facilities became venues for public services including insurance and social security functions. The market discipline that privatisation was intended to introduce was partially absorbed by the continuing public service obligations that a nationwide network in a declining-population country could not escape.

The structural summary of Post 039: Postal privatisation addressed a genuine RBM dysfunction — the automatic flow of household capital into politically-allocated projects without market discipline. The FILP reform of 2001 was the more structurally effective intervention: it broke the mandatory pipeline. Koizumi's 2005 election deployed CPM simplification to overcome the RSM blockage that had prevented reform for decades. The long-run GMM outcome is ambiguous: Japan Post Bank found new channels for the capital it accumulated; the public service logic of a nationwide network in a low-density rural Japan continued to pull the institution back toward public functions. The reform succeeded politically and partially economically. The structural tension between commercial operation and universal service obligation remains unresolved — and may be inherently unresolvable in a country whose population distribution makes universal coverage commercially unviable.

Post 040 — International Peace Cooperation Law

While the financial and postal systems were being reformed, a different kind of institutional architecture was being built. The 1992 International Peace Cooperation Law (PKO法) redefined what Japan's Self-Defence Forces were legally permitted to do. That redefinition had an unexpected consequence three years later, when a magnitude 7.3 earthquake struck Kobe at 5:46 in the morning of 17 January 1995.
