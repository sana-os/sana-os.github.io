Post 051 — Case Study · Agricultural Policy

Japan's agricultural mystery: why the most protective policy produced the most competitive decline

Japan spent sixty years applying some of the world's most intensive agricultural support — and produced declining food self-sufficiency, aging farming communities, and internationally uncompetitive farm structures. The SANA analysis of why the policy designed to prevent this outcome reliably produced it.

Post 050 examined gentan — the rice acreage reduction programme that paid farmers not to grow rice for fifty years. Its structural conclusion: a governance system built for wartime scarcity became a structural liability in peacetime abundance, was maintained by the coupling of Decider electoral interests with cooperative constituency management, and was ultimately broken not by rational policy argument but by the simultaneous external forcing functions of 1993.

Post 051 examines what surrounded gentan: the broader architecture of Japanese agricultural policy that produced the outcome its designers explicitly sought to prevent. The 1961 Basic Agricultural Act aimed to create a modern, competitive agricultural sector with larger farms, diversified production, and productivity comparable to industrial wages. Instead, Japan spent the next sixty years operating a system that maintained small-scale farming, suppressed agricultural consolidation, and produced steadily declining food self-sufficiency — while spending more per agricultural worker on subsidies and protection than almost any other developed economy.

This is what agricultural economists call Japan's agricultural mystery: the policy that was most intensively applied produced the outcome it was most explicitly designed to avoid. Understanding why requires examining all four SANA dimensions simultaneously.

GMM: three basic laws and what each one failed to accomplish

Japan's postwar agricultural governance can be periodised by three foundational laws, each representing a distinct GMM configuration, each diagnosed correctly before it was enacted, and each failing in the specific way that its critics predicted.

The 1961 Basic Agricultural Act (農業基本法) was the high-growth era's attempt to transform Japanese agriculture from a wartime food supply system into a modern competitive industry. Its two central objectives were "selective expansion" (選択的拡大) — shifting production from surplus rice toward vegetables, livestock, and fruit where domestic demand was growing — and "structural reform" (構造改革) — consolidating small farms into larger units capable of mechanised production at internationally competitive cost. The GMM logic was sound: the Layer A energy security problem required an efficient domestic food industry, and the Layer C institutional architecture needed to enable it.

The structural failure was in the interaction between the reform's logic and the economic incentives that actually confronted farmers. High-growth industrialisation produced rapid land price appreciation: agricultural land near expanding cities became more valuable held than sold. Simultaneously, the development of small-scale agricultural machinery (rice transplanters, compact combine harvesters) made part-time farming economically viable: a household could maintain paddy fields as a weekend activity while earning full industrial wages during the week. These two dynamics — asset appreciation and mechanisation — made it irrational for the vast majority of Japanese farmers to exit agriculture, which was precisely what structural reform required. The reform failed not because the analysis was wrong, but because the economic environment in which it was implemented defeated its own preconditions.

The 1999 Basic Act on Food, Agriculture and Rural Areas (食料・農業・農村基本法) was enacted after the 1993 harvest failure and GATT Uruguay Round had forced the opening of the rice market. Its GMM advance over the 1961 law was significant: it acknowledged that agriculture performed functions beyond commodity production — watershed protection, landscape maintenance, rural community preservation, biodiversity stewardship — and codified these "multifunctional" roles as objects of public support. This was a genuine conceptual advance: it created the legal basis for direct income payments to farmers (decoupled from production volumes) that replaced the price support mechanism the WTO regime was progressively constraining.

The 1999 law's structural limitation was that it preserved the production volume decline by treating agriculture's multifunctional value as a reason to maintain farming rather than a reason to restructure farming. Supporting rural community preservation through agricultural subsidy maintained small-scale farming not to make food production competitive but to keep landscapes inhabited and community services viable. This is a legitimate policy objective — but it is not a food security policy, and the continued decline in food self-sufficiency over the subsequent two decades reflected the gap between the law's stated priorities and its operational logic.

The 2024 revised Basic Act (食料・農業・農村基本法 改正) is the most structurally significant reform since 1961 — and the one that most clearly reflects the external forcing function logic that runs throughout this blog's arc. The external environment that produced it: the 2019–2020 COVID supply chain disruptions, the 2022 Ukraine war's impact on fertiliser and feed grain prices, the persistent decline in Japanese food self-sufficiency to approximately 38% on a caloric basis. The 2024 revision explicitly prioritises food security in normal times (平時からの食料安全保障) rather than treating food security as a crisis scenario. It mandates cost pass-through pricing (合理的な費用の考慮) — the legal acknowledgement that food prices in Japan must reflect actual production costs, including rising input costs, rather than being artificially suppressed for consumer political reasons. And it formally incorporates food system actors beyond farmers — processors, distributors, retailers — into the governance framework, recognising that food security requires the entire supply chain, not just the farm gate.

RSM: the iron triangle and its transformation

The governance failure that sustained Japan's agricultural mystery for sixty years is the RSM configuration that Post 050 began to describe: the "iron triangle" of LDP agricultural politicians, the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (農林水産省, MAFF), and the agricultural cooperative network (JA).

The iron triangle was not created by corruption or design failure. It emerged from the functional logic of a democratic system in which rural votes were structurally overweighted by electoral geography, agricultural policy was technically complex enough to require specialist bureaucratic capacity, and the cooperative network was the only organisation capable of delivering policy implementation at the scale of millions of small farming households simultaneously. Each node of the triangle was performing its designated function: politicians managing constituencies, bureaucrats managing complex regulatory systems, and cooperatives delivering agricultural services and organising farmer interests.

The RSM failure was in what the triangle's internal logic excluded. The Auditor function — independent evaluation of whether agricultural policy was achieving its stated objectives — was structurally marginalised. Finance Ministry interventions were consistently overridden by agricultural political pressure. Consumer interests in affordable food were systematically underweighted against producer interests in price support. International trade exposure was framed as a threat to be managed rather than competitive information about Japan's agricultural efficiency gap. The correction loop existed formally in the institutions of democratic governance; it did not function substantively because the political cost of correction was systematically higher than the fiscal cost of continuation.

JA's specific role warrants examination. The cooperative network's original Executor function — providing shared services to small farmers who could not individually afford them — was functionally appropriate for the postwar agricultural economy it served. But as the farming population declined and aged, JA's institutional interests diverged from those of the farmers it nominally served. JA's financial businesses (banking, insurance) had grown to a scale where they generated the majority of the network's revenue. These businesses were most profitably served by large numbers of farming households maintaining accounts and policies, regardless of whether those households were competitive agricultural operators. JA's Integrator function in policy implementation — which gave it control over subsidy allocation and compliance monitoring — translated this organisational interest into structural resistance to the consolidation and restructuring that would have reduced its membership base.

The RSM transformation that the 2024 law initiates is significant but incomplete. The formal dismantling of the iron triangle's most rigid features had been gradual: the 1994 electoral reform reduced rural vote overweighting; the Koizumi government's 2000s deregulation began opening agricultural land access to corporate operators; the 2018 gentan abolition removed the cooperative network's role in production volume management. But the cooperative network's financial and insurance businesses remain large, its political presence in agricultural policy deliberation remains substantial, and the structural transition from price-supported small farming to commercially competitive larger-scale operations remains far from complete.

RBM: the resource allocation distortions and their consequences

The RBM analysis of Japanese agricultural policy requires examining three interconnected resource allocation distortions that the iron triangle governance produced and that explain the pattern of declining self-sufficiency despite increasing policy support.

The first distortion was rice over-specialisation. High rice price support made rice the dominant rational crop choice for Japanese farmers throughout the price support era. The subsidies for converting paddy to other crops — the economic logic of gentan — were designed to offset this distortion but were insufficient to overcome it. The result: as rice consumption per capita declined and other commodity imports grew, Japan maintained large-scale rice production capability while underdeveloping the production capability for the crops that Japanese consumers were actually eating more of. Food self-sufficiency declined not because Japan was producing less food in absolute terms but because it was producing the wrong food relative to actual consumption patterns.

The second distortion was the small farm trap. Price support at well above world market levels removed the productivity penalty from small-scale inefficient farming. A household farming 0.5 hectares of paddy could remain solvent because rice prices were 5–8 times world market levels; at world market prices, that same household would be unable to cover input costs. The price support therefore actively sustained the farm structure that structural reform was trying to eliminate. This is the central irony of Japan's agricultural mystery: the policy that was most effective at protecting farmers was simultaneously the policy that most effectively prevented the structural transformation that would have made Japanese farming internationally competitive.

The third distortion was the supply chain isolation. Because Japanese agricultural prices were so far above world market levels, the entire domestic food supply chain was organised around this artificially high price floor. Processors, distributors, and retailers developed business models premised on expensive domestic ingredients. Investment in food processing technology, distribution efficiency, and retail innovation was constrained by the structural cost disadvantage built into the input price. When the WTO regime progressively constrained price support and import barriers, the supply chain lacked the efficiency advantages that would have offset the competitiveness loss. The 2024 law's inclusion of food system actors in the agricultural governance framework is partly a recognition that agricultural competitiveness requires supply chain transformation, not just farm-level reform.

CPM: the village as a cognitive lock on agricultural modernisation

The CPM analysis of Japanese agricultural policy extends the analysis of Post 050's village collective responsibility mechanism to the broader question of why agricultural modernisation — which individual farmers in many cases rationally understood to be necessary — was collectively resisted for decades.

Japan's agricultural villages maintained a social structure that rewarded conformity and penalised differentiation in ways that went beyond the formal gentan compliance mechanism. The shared infrastructure of rice agriculture — irrigation systems, pest management, drainage maintenance — required cooperation at the village level regardless of whether gentan existed. This functional interdependence created genuine collective action dynamics: a farmer who adopted dramatically different cultivation practices disrupted the shared schedule of water management, pest control, and harvest timing on which all village members depended.

The CPM mechanism that operated in this context was not primarily the fear of formal social sanction — the "murahachibu" exclusion that Post 050 described — but the prior cognitive step: the suppression of the capacity to imagine alternatives. Farmers who had inherited paddy land from parents who had inherited it from grandparents, who had always grown rice, who lived in communities where everyone grew rice, who organised their working year around the rice calendar — these farmers did not experience the decision to continue growing rice as a choice. It was the default. Agricultural modernisation required not just removing the formal barriers to change (price support, production mandates, land consolidation restrictions) but creating the cognitive infrastructure within which different choices could be imagined. That infrastructure — exposure to market price signals, access to information about alternative crops and markets, experience of successfully operating outside the subsidy framework — was systematically absent from farming communities precisely because the subsidy system had removed the necessity of developing it.

The cases of successful agricultural transformation that have emerged since the 2018 gentan abolition — the JAとなみ野 onion specialisation, the emergence of large-scale corporate farm operations in Hokkaido, the export-oriented premium fruit producers in various prefectures — share a common CPM feature: they were initiated by operators who had some form of exposure to external reference points that made the alternative visible. Second-generation farmers who had worked in other industries before returning to farming. Regions where geography made rice cultivation marginally viable and market alternatives genuinely competitive. Agricultural management programmes that provided the analytical tools for business-model thinking. The cognitive transformation that policy change enabled was not automatic; it required the development of the cognitive infrastructure that decades of administered prices had prevented from forming.

The 2024 revision in structural context: what has actually changed

The 2024 Basic Act revision is the most comprehensive restructuring of Japan's agricultural governance architecture since 1961. Its structural significance goes beyond the specific provisions it contains to the underlying shift in the GMM configuration it represents.

Before 2024, Japanese agricultural governance was organised around protecting producers from market exposure. Price support, import barriers, subsidy systems, and production management all operated in the same direction: insulating Japanese farmers from the competitive signals that would have required structural adaptation. The system justified itself partly through food security arguments (domestic production is more reliable than import dependence) and partly through multifunctionality arguments (agricultural communities provide non-market environmental and social services). Both arguments are genuinely valid in some form. Neither provides a rationale for protecting prices at 5–8 times world market levels indefinitely.

The 2024 revision shifts the GMM configuration in three structurally important ways. First, it defines food security operationally rather than rhetorically — specifying the supply chain redundancy, stockpile levels, and import diversification that actual food security requires, rather than using food security as a general justification for production support. Second, it mandates cost-reflective pricing — acknowledging that food prices must cover actual production costs if farming is to remain economically viable, which is a direct rejection of the political logic that suppressed rice prices for consumer constituencies while simultaneously supporting farmers through subsidy. Third, it includes the entire food system in its governance scope — the recognition that food security is a system property, not a farm-gate property, and that supply chain actors bear responsibilities as well as rights.

What the 2024 revision does not do is equally important. It does not specify the competitive pathway for Japanese agriculture in world markets — the mechanism by which specific crops, in specific regions, with specific production systems, achieve international competitiveness. Japan's export success in premium agricultural products (wagyu beef, premium rice varieties, specialty fruit, sake) is real and growing, but it represents a small fraction of total agricultural production. The structural question — what happens to the majority of Japanese agricultural land that is growing rice at prices still above world market levels — remains open. The 2024 law created the governance architecture for asking that question and acting on the answer. Whether the answer is found depends on factors the law cannot itself produce: the cognitive transformation in farming communities, the supply chain investment in competitive processing and distribution, and the continued external pressure from world market prices that makes the alternative to transformation visible.

Japan's agricultural mystery — intensive protection producing declining competitiveness — is not a mystery when examined through the SANA framework. It is the predictable output of a governance configuration in which the Decider's electoral interests were coupled to the Executor's institutional interests in ways that made the Auditor's correct diagnosis systematically irrelevant to policy outcomes. The 2024 revision has changed the governance configuration. Whether it changes the outcomes depends on whether the external forcing functions that history has consistently required are present — or whether Japanese agriculture, for the first time, restructures itself in the absence of crisis.
The structural connection to Post 052: Posts 050 and 051 complete the food policy arc's domestic dimension — gentan and the broader agricultural governance failure. Post 052 begins the fisheries policy arc, which has a parallel but distinct structural history. Where agricultural governance failed through Decider-Executor coupling in the domestic electoral system, fisheries governance failed through a different mechanism: the historical accumulation of community-based resource management rights that made scientific stock management institutionally impossible. The structural analysis is different; the outcome — resource depletion through governance failure — is the same.
